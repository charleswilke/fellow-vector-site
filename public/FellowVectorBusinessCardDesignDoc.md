Fellow Vector Business Card Design Doc

Project Summary

Create a business card for Fellow Vector that feels like a tactile extension of the website: warm, human, practical, retro-modern, and quietly confident. The card should position Fellow Vector as a thoughtful AI consulting practice rather than a generic technology vendor.

The centerpiece is the hand-drawn portrait of Charles: simplified, expressive, approachable, and editorial. The portrait should not feel like a corporate headshot. It should feel like a human signal inside a world of synthetic noise.

Core Design Idea

A retro consulting artifact for the AI era.

The card should feel like something from a 1970s strategy bureau, a French pop-art film poster, or a slightly strange but highly competent advisory firm. It should be charming without being unserious, designed without looking overproduced, and personal without becoming vanity-driven.

The best version feels like a collectible calling card: warm cream paper, black ink illustration, muted orange geometry, and sparse, confident information architecture.

Design Principles

1. Human First

The portrait is the emotional anchor. Fellow Vector is not selling abstract AI hype; it is selling judgment, translation, taste, and practical guidance. The portrait should make the business feel personal and grounded.

2. Strategic, Not Techy

Avoid the obvious AI visual clichés: glowing blue gradients, circuit boards, robot hands, neural nets, chrome spheres, or generic futuristic UI motifs. The card should feel more like a trusted creative strategist than a SaaS booth giveaway.

3. Retro Warmth, Modern Restraint

Use retro color, shape, and texture, but keep the composition clean. The card should have enough negative space to feel premium and enough personality to feel unmistakably Fellow Vector.

4. Artifact Energy

This should feel like a physical object with intention: something you would want to keep on your desk, pin to a board, or photograph next to a notebook. Texture, paper stock, and print method matter.

Recommended Format

Primary Recommendation: Vertical Two-Sided Card

A vertical orientation gives the portrait more presence and makes the card feel less expected. It also lets the design behave more like a character card, author card, or small poster.

Standard size: 2 in × 3.5 in
Orientation: Portrait / vertical
Corners: Optional slight radius, but square corners may feel more editorial and classic
Paper: Heavy uncoated cream stock
Finish: Matte, tactile, not glossy

Front Side Concept

Purpose

The front should act as the brand-facing side: clear, memorable, and sparse. This is where Fellow Vector gets introduced as a practice.

Layout

* Warm cream background
* Large Fellow Vector wordmark or text lockup
* Small supporting line beneath
* Optional geometric accent or signal-line motif
* Minimal contact or no contact information

FELLOW VECTOR

Alternate tagline options:

Visual Treatment

The front can be typography-led. The Fellow Vector name should feel sturdy and slightly retro. It does not need to scream. A small orange geometric mark, underline, signal dash, or vector-like directional accent can tie the front to the portrait side.

Back Side Concept

Purpose

The back should be the personal calling card: Charles as the face of the practice, with essential contact details.

Layout

* Cream background filling the entire card
* Simplified portrait of Charles, cropped head-and-shoulders
* One large orange geometric shape behind the portrait
* Contact details arranged in a tight stack
* Optional short descriptor line

Portrait Direction

Use the existing illustration as the basis, but simplify it further for small-format printing:

* Preserve the expressive eyes and round glasses
* Keep the beard and hair as bold ink masses, not over-detailed hatching
* Reduce shirt pattern complexity
* Make the shoulders clean and readable
* Let the orange geometric shape sit behind the head and shoulders
* Avoid a circular portrait frame; the cream background should be the full card background

The portrait should feel hand-drawn, editorial, and warm. It should not feel like a vectorized app avatar or a corporate illustration library asset.

Composition Options

Option A: Vertical Portrait Card

Best overall direction.

Top half: portrait, slightly oversized, with orange geometric shape behind it.
Bottom half: name, role, service descriptors, and contact details.

This option gives the card personality and makes it feel collectible without sacrificing clarity.

Strengths

* Most memorable
* Strong use of portrait
* Feels unique to Fellow Vector
* Works well as a leave-behind after conversations or workshops

Watchouts

* Needs careful hierarchy so the portrait does not overpower the information
* Small text must remain readable

Option B: Horizontal Split Card

Left side: portrait and geometric accent.
Right side: name, title, services, and contact info.

This is safer and more conventional while still feeling distinctive.

Strengths

* Practical and familiar
* Easy to scan
* Strong professional read

Watchouts

* Less special than the vertical version
* Could feel more like a standard consultant card if the typography is too conservative

Option C: Poster-Back Card

Front: full portrait with Fellow Vector name integrated almost like a small poster.
Back: clean contact information only.

This is the most artful version.

Strengths

* Very memorable
* Strongest object quality
* Feels like an extension of the Fellow Vector visual world

Watchouts

* Slightly less immediately business-card-like
* Requires confident printing and careful balance

Color Palette

Core Colors

Warm Cream
Primary background color. Should feel like aged paper, not beige office stationery.

Ink Black / Soft Black
Primary linework and text. Avoid pure digital black if possible; use a softened ink black.

Muted Orange
Primary geometric accent. This should connect to the existing portrait and Fellow Vector’s warm retro palette.

Brick Red
Secondary accent, used sparingly.

Muted Yellow / Ochre
Optional small highlight or rule line.

Color Philosophy

Use color as structure, not decoration. The palette should feel warm, editorial, and intentional. The orange shape should be the main visual signal; other colors should support it, not compete with it.

Typography Direction

Brand Name

The Fellow Vector wordmark should feel:

* Retro but not kitschy
* Confident but not loud
* Slightly geometric
* Human-scale
* Legible at small sizes

Potential typographic moods:

* 1970s corporate training manual
* French pop-art poster typography
* Mid-century technical manual
* Slightly condensed grotesk
* Warm serif/sans pairing

Body Text

Contact details should be clean and highly readable. A simple sans-serif or typewriter-adjacent face could work well, but avoid anything too novelty.

Hierarchy

1. Fellow Vector
2. Charles Wilke

Illustration Treatment

Keep

* Round glasses
* Expressive eyes
* Friendly but knowing expression
* Hair shape and beard silhouette
* Hand-drawn ink quality
* Orange geometric shape
* Cream background

Simplify

* Shirt pattern
* Fine hair hatching
* Tiny facial linework
* Shoulder details
* Any overly delicate strokes that could disappear in print

Avoid

* Photorealistic rendering
* Cartoon exaggeration
* Overly polished vector avatar look
* Circular frame around portrait
* Too many background shapes
* Any AI-themed icons or symbols

Print & Production Recommendations

Paper

Use a heavy, uncoated cream stock. Something with tooth would reinforce the handmade/editorial feeling.

Recommended feel:

* 16 pt or thicker
* Uncoated or matte
* Warm white / natural cream
* Slight texture if available

Finish

Avoid gloss. A matte finish will preserve the tactile, retro quality.

Optional premium details:

* Spot color orange
* Letterpress-style impression for the wordmark
* Slight deboss on Fellow Vector
* Rounded corners only if they feel intentional, not startup-default

Print Considerations

The portrait should be tested at actual size before printing. Fine hatching may need to be thickened. The shirt lines in particular should be reduced or simplified so they do not become visual noise.

Why This Works

This direction makes the card feel personal, strategic, and unmistakably connected to the Fellow Vector world. It avoids the generic visual language of AI consulting while still communicating competence. The portrait creates trust. The geometry creates structure. The copy creates clarity.

The resulting card should feel like Charles is handing someone a small artifact of his point of view: warm, sharp, human, and useful.
